## Where It Started

It all started with a video by Niklas Christl — [DOPAMINE DETOX | How To Take Back Control Over Your Life](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bV_NdUZEmnE).

He did a 7-day dopamine detox himself, with rules: no social media, no digital entertainment, no junk food, no alcohol, no porn, no music. It made a lot of sense to me, so I decided to follow his rules and try it out myself.

Honestly, I'd tried a few times before and never made it through. This time I'm treating it as a proper challenge and tracking it on my [personal site](https://guancioul.github.io/#/challenges/dopamine-detox).

## Why I Wanted to Do This

It started when I was going through Scott H. Young's ultralearning system, trying to build a study plan using his methods.

Simple enough in theory, but I couldn't get through my daily tasks.

After thinking about it, I realized the problem wasn't the plan itself. I'm home most of the time, and my phone is always within reach — scroll whenever, YouTube whenever. All of that adds up and the day just disappears. The issue wasn't effort. I just couldn't focus for more than a few minutes at a time.

## The Problem with Dopamine

Dopamine isn't about enjoyment — it's about wanting more. Every time you scroll, get a notification, or see something new, your brain releases a little dopamine and you want to keep going. But the satisfaction you get each time is a little less than the last, so you need more and more stimulation just to feel okay.

There's a metaphor in *Dopamine Nation* that I think nails it: there's a seesaw in your brain, with pleasure on one side and pain on the other. Every time you get pleasure, the seesaw tips that way — but your body compensates by adding weight to the pain side to restore balance. So when the stimulation is gone, you don't land back at neutral. You're below it. To get back to balance, you need more pleasure — and then pain gets another weight added. The loop continues, you need more stimulation each time, and you feel less from it.

Modern apps are built exactly around this. Infinite scroll, notifications, algorithms — all designed to keep you stuck in that loop. It's not a willpower problem. Your brain is up against a system specifically engineered to exploit it.

Over time, low-stimulation things — reading, writing, just sitting and thinking — start to feel boring because they can't compete with what you've trained your brain to expect.

## Why Seven Days

Seven days isn't a scientific number, but it's long enough to feel a real difference and short enough that it feels survivable. Saying "never use social media again" is an easy way to quit before you start. Saying "just seven days" makes it something you can actually finish.

## What I'm Cutting Out

- Social media
- Digital entertainment (video, games)
- Junk food
- Alcohol
- Porn
- Music

The last one always gets a reaction. What's wrong with music? The way I see it, music is another way to make boredom tolerable — commuting, doing dishes, running, there's almost never a moment of just being quiet. Cutting music out is forcing myself to actually sit in that discomfort.

That said, complete silence is hard too, so I'm using an app called **Tide** for white noise instead. White noise is different from music — it doesn't pull your attention anywhere, it just softens the background. Tide also has guided meditation sessions, which I've been using to fill the time I'd normally spend on my phone.

I also gave myself a **two days rule**, a concept I picked up from Matt D'Avella: you can't miss the same item two days in a row. One slip is fine, but not two consecutive days. That way, a single bad day doesn't feel like the whole thing is ruined.

## Why I'm Actually Doing This

I'm not trying to give these things up forever. I don't think scrolling your phone or listening to music is inherently bad.

What I want is to get clear on where these things fit in my life. Am I choosing to use them, or am I just reaching for them automatically whenever I'm bored? Before this challenge, I couldn't honestly answer that.

Seven days. Force myself out of that default state, and see what's left when all of it is gone.
