## What it is (for me)

A **7-day break** from high-stimulation habits — not forever abstinence. The goal is to reset tolerance for boredom and notice when I'm reaching for stimulation on autopilot instead of by choice.

Inspired by [Niklas Christl's 7-day detox](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bV_NdUZEmnE). I've tried before and not finished; this playbook exists so the next attempt starts from notes, not from zero.

## The checklist (daily)

Every day, all six:

- [ ] No social media
- [ ] No digital entertainment (video, games)
- [ ] No junk food
- [ ] No alcohol & drugs
- [ ] No porn
- [ ] No music

Copy the checklist into a new challenge entry each day — see [challenge template](/challenges/dopamine-detox).

## Two-day rule

**No single item can be missed two days in a row.** (From Matt D'Avella.)

One slip is fine. Two consecutive misses on the same item means the detox is drifting — reset that item the next day instead of treating the whole week as failed.

## Music & white noise

Music is on the list because it's my default way to avoid silence — commute, chores, running, almost never just quiet.

Complete silence is hard, so I use **Tide** for white noise (not music — no melody pulling attention). Tide's guided meditations also fill gaps I'd otherwise spend on the phone.

## Why seven days

Not magic — just **long enough to feel a difference, short enough to start**. "Never use social media again" is easy to abandon before day one. "Just seven days" is finishable.

## Core idea (quick recap)

- Dopamine drives **wanting**, not lasting satisfaction — each hit feels a little weaker, so you chase more stimulation.
- Apps are built to exploit this (infinite scroll, notifications, algorithms). It's not purely a willpower problem.
- After enough overstimulation, low-key activities (reading, writing, sitting with thoughts) feel boring because the baseline is skewed.

For the full narrative and *Dopamine Nation* seesaw metaphor, read the [blog post](/blog/why-dopamine-detox).

## When it gets hard

From past runs and the current challenge:

- **Middle days** are often the hardest — day 1 has momentum; later days feel flat or irritable.
- **Home + phone in reach** is the main environment trap — not the plan itself.
- **External stress** (bad weather, tooling frustration, etc.) pushes toward old escapes — expect that, don't treat it as proof the detox failed.

## After the week

I'm not trying to quit these things forever. The question to answer honestly:

> Am I choosing this, or reaching for it because I'm bored?

Reintroduce deliberately — one category at a time if needed — and notice which ones pull back into autopilot.
