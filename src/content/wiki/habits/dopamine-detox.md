For a stretch, cut back on high-stimulation inputs — social media, short video, junk food — and get used to quieter days again. Runs are usually days or weeks, not forever; "detox" is metaphorical too. You can't purge dopamine from your brain. The real question is: **am I choosing this, or reaching for it on autopilot when bored?**

## The DOPAMINE framework

*Dopamine Nation* ([*Finding Balance in the Age of Indulgence*](https://www.amazon.com/Dopamine-Nation-Finding-Balance-Indulgence/dp/152474672X)) is by Stanford psychiatrist Anna Lembke. I first came across its ideas — pleasure-pain balance, a skewed baseline, chasing pleasure — in [帅soserious's 【快感上瘾?】如何欺骗大脑做困难的事!](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ymZ_NzZHaQM); *Dopamine Nation* is listed in the video description as a main reference.

The book uses patient stories and neuroscience to explain how chasing high stimulation raises your baseline until low-key activities feel boring. Lembke organizes an eight-step process that spells **DOPAMINE**:

| Letter | Term | What it means |
|--------|------|---------------|
| **D** | Data | **Track it** — what you use, how much, how often, and what triggers it |
| **O** | Objectives | **Why you use it** — fun, boredom, anxiety, social pressure, escape? |
| **P** | Problems | **The cost** — what harm does it cause? Do you want this life in one or five years? |
| **A** | Abstinence | **Stop completely** for a stretch so the baseline can reset; the book often cites **~30 days** (the first two weeks are usually the hardest) |
| **M** | Mindfulness | **Sit with urges** during abstinence — observe discomfort without reaching for the old fix |
| **I** | Insight | **See clearly** — only after abstaining can you tell what you actually need vs. what was autopilot |
| **N** | Next steps | **Decide what's next** — keep abstaining, or try moderation? Write a specific plan |
| **E** | Experiment | **Test in the real world** — find what amounts and contexts still feel like a choice; failures are data |

Dopamine drives **wanting more**, not lasting satisfaction. Every scroll, notification, or new hit releases a little dopamine and you want to keep going, but each time satisfies a little less, so you chase more stimulation just to feel okay.

Lembke's **seesaw** metaphor: pleasure on one side, pain on the other. Each hit of pleasure tips the board, but your body adds weight to the pain side to restore balance — so when stimulation stops, you land **below** neutral, not at it. You need more pleasure to rebalance; pain gets another weight. The loop continues: more stimulation needed, less felt each time.

Modern apps are built around this — infinite scroll, notifications, algorithms. It's not purely a willpower problem. Over time, low-stimulation things feel boring because the baseline has been raised.

## Common variants

The main difference between approaches is **Abstinence (A)** — what to cut and for how long:

### Lembke / full DOPAMINE process

All eight steps, abstinence typically **~30 days**, focused on **one** behavior that's causing trouble (social media, food, alcohol, etc.). Best if you want a structured understanding of why the habit stuck and how to live after the reset.

### Niklas Christl — 7-day version

[YouTube 7-day detox](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bV_NdUZEmnE) — fixed **7 days**, **six rules**, all at once:

- No social media
- No digital entertainment (video, games)
- No junk food
- No alcohol & drugs
- No porn
- No music

Cut all high stimulation at once — doesn't walk through the full DOPAMINE steps. Good if you want a clear checklist and a short, finishable run. Seven days isn't scientific, but it's **long enough to feel a difference, short enough to start** — "never use social media again" is easy to abandon before day one; "just seven days" is finishable.

### Cameron Sepah — Dopamine Fasting 2.0

Psychiatrist's [CBT-based approach](https://medium.com/swlh/dopamine-fasting-2-0-the-hot-silicon-valley-trend-7c4dc3ba2213) — **not** about lowering dopamine, and **not** about avoiding all pleasure:

- Only restrict **behaviors that are problematic for you** (emotional eating, excessive internet, shopping, etc.)
- **Time-boxed** abstinence: e.g. last 1–4 hours of each day, one weekend day, one weekend per quarter, one week per year
- Built on CBT stimulus control and exposure & response prevention

Good if a full six-item cold turkey isn't realistic, but you still want scheduled breaks from impulsive behavior.

## Two-day rule

A **tolerance strategy** often paired with detox or challenges — not a detox variant itself, but a way to finish abstinence without all-or-nothing thinking.

**No single item missed two days in a row.** One slip is fine; two consecutive misses on the same item starts a new "not doing it" pattern and the whole run starts to drift.

The idea comes from James Clear's [*Atomic Habits*](https://jamesclear.com/atomic-habits) — **never miss twice** (missing once is an accident; missing twice is the start of a new habit). [Chris Kyle's write-up](https://medium.com/@ChrisKyle/the-two-day-rule-the-counter-intuitive-habit-strategy-that-actually-works-90982e9ec78d) explains it in plain language.

## After abstinence: Next steps & experiment

In Lembke's DOPAMINE framework, **Abstinence through Insight** is the abstinence period itself; **Next steps (N)** and **Experiment (E)** are what come **after**. In an [interview](https://www.psychiatryadvisor.com/features/interview-author-dopamine-nation-finding-balance-age-of-indulgence/), she describes reviewing what worked and what didn't, then deciding whether to keep abstaining or try moderation — the key is a **specific plan** (how much, how often, in what contexts). **Experiment** means taking your reset baseline back into daily life and testing that plan; failed moderation is still data, and may show that abstinence is the better long-term choice.

Many detox variants (including short checklist runs) emphasize the abstinence window only, which makes it easy to add everything back the day it ends. The parallel you hear elsewhere holds up:

- **Sprint retrospective** — at the end of each sprint, Scrum teams inspect and adapt and pick improvements for the next cycle; [Scrum.org](https://www.scrum.org/resources/blog/skipping-retrospectives-making-your-scrum-work-17) treats retros as core to continuous improvement — skip them and the same problems repeat.
- **Study plans and habit cycles** — reviewing what worked and what to change after one full round beats rushing straight into the next without closing the loop.

After abstinence ends, write a short retro:

- **Insight** — what did abstinence clarify? Which urges were stronger or weaker than expected?
- **Next steps** — for **each** behavior: keep abstaining, reintroduce in moderation, or watch and wait? If moderating, rules need to be concrete enough to follow.
- **Experiment** — what will you test over the next few weeks? What counts as success, and what triggers a plan change?
- **Next round** — if you run abstinence again: what to cut, for how long, and what environmental changes to make?

Without this step, abstinence is usually a temporary reset, not lasting change.
